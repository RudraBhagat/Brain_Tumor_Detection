Types of Brain Tumors

Glioma

Most common type of primary brain tumor. Can be benign or malignant. Includes astrocytomas, oligodendrogliomas, and glioblastomas.

~50% of all brain tumors
Grade: I-IV (IV being most aggressive)
Common in: Adults aged 45-65
5-year survival: 5-35% depending on grade

Meningioma

Usually benign tumors that arise from the meninges. Most common in women and older adults. Often asymptomatic.

~30% of all brain tumors
Grade: I-III (Most are grade I)
Common in: Women aged 40-70
5-year survival: 70-95% depending on grade

Pituitary

Tumors that develop in the pituitary gland. Can affect hormone production. Most are benign adenomas.

~15% of all brain tumors
Grade: I-IV
Common in: Adults aged 30-50
5-year survival: 80-95% depending on type

Other Types

Includes schwannomas, medulloblastomas, and metastatic tumors from other parts of the body.

~5% of all brain tumors
Grade: Varies by type
Common in: All age groups
5-year survival: Varies significantly

Risk Factors

Ionizing Radiation

Exposure to high doses of ionizing radiation, particularly to the head and neck area.

Genetic Factors

Certain inherited genetic conditions like Neurofibromatosis, Li-Fraumeni syndrome, and Turcot syndrome.

Gender

Meningiomas are more common in women, while gliomas are slightly more common in men.

Age

Risk increases with age, with most brain tumors diagnosed in people over 65.

Environmental Factors

Exposure to certain chemicals, pesticides, and electromagnetic fields may increase risk.

Cell Phone Use

Some studies suggest long-term heavy cell phone use may be associated with increased risk.

Diagnosis Methods

MRI (Magnetic Resonance Imaging)

The gold standard for brain tumor diagnosis. Provides detailed images of brain structures and can detect tumors as small as a few millimeters.

  • High-resolution imaging
  • No radiation exposure
  • Can show tumor characteristics

CT Scan

Useful for detecting bleeding, calcification, or bone involvement. Often used in emergency situations.

  • Fast imaging
  • Good for acute symptoms
  • Uses ionizing radiation

Biopsy

Surgical removal of a small tissue sample for microscopic examination to determine tumor type and grade.

  • Definitive diagnosis
  • Determines treatment approach
  • Minimally invasive techniques available

Neurological Examination

Assessment of cognitive function, motor skills, sensory perception, and reflexes to evaluate tumor impact.

  • Evaluates functional impact
  • Helps determine urgency
  • Monitors disease progression
